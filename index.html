Crime In Denver

A visualiztion by Nick Reinicke

Crime is an unavoidable feature of any city. What does it look like in the city of Denver, Colorado specifically? To answer this question, let's take a look at all of the recorded crime between the years of 2013 and 2017 within the city limits. We'll begin by breaking out all of the crime events by type. We see that the most common event is Public Disorder and the least common event is Murder (this is much better than the other way around). Hover over each bar to get the exact number of recorded events.

Crime By Type (2013-2017)

Next, let's inspect how the prevalence of crime has varied over the recorded time frame. To do this, we'll look at the ratio of the total number of crime events to the population of Denver. Visualizing this particular metric, we see that crime has actually decreased over the time period of the year 2013 to 2017. Hover over each data point to get the exact metric.

Crime Over Time (2013-2017)

Crime Calendar (2013)

Let's continue to look at crime over time but with a sharper focus. To do this, the occurrence of crime events in the years 2013-2017 have been aggregated per day. Based on this metric, a color has been assigned to each day with darker colors representing more crimes occurring on that day. Some interesting things to note: It appears that crime could be increasing during the summer months. This trend is not apparent for the remainder of the years, leading one to suspect that perhaps the certain crimes were not recorded at the start of the year considering that this is the first year they were recorded in this manner. Another interesting feature is that we can clearly distinguish Christmas Day by it's markedly lighter color. Criminals apparently have more important things to do on the holiday.

Crime Calendar (2014)

Again, we can see that significantly less crime occurs on Christmas day.

Crime Calendar (2015)

In 2015, we see similar trends for the Christmas and Thanksgiving. Also note that there was significantly less crime recorded on February 22nd, 2015. I was unable to find a suitable explanation. I would love to hear from any reader that has a theory as to why this anomaly might appear.

Crime Calendar (2016)

Note here that it appears the weekends experience marginally less crime rates than the weekdays.

Crime Calendar (2017)

Finally, in 2017, we observe similar trends with respect to the previous years. We see that the Thanksgiving and Christmas holidays are tied to a significantly lower crime rate. In addition, we see bands of lower crime rates on the the weekends. What other trends are visible?

About This Visualization

Narrative Structure

The hybrid narrative structure chosen for this visualization is the interactive slideshow structure. The users are presented with a series of scenes that they can investigate in various ways. A visual template with a fixed color scheme is used to maintain consistency.

Scenes

This visualization has been organized into discrete scenes and allows the viewer to scroll through all of the scenes from top to bottom (the viewer can also click on the navigation bar to go directly to a scene). Each scene presents a new dimension of the data and gives the viewer details on demand in certain instances. The order was chosen to give the user an overview and then dive down into interesting characteristics.

Annotations

Annotations are used throughout the visualization to draw attention to specific interesting elements. Each slide contains text that highlights various aspects of the data that is being displayed. In addition, certain slides have direct annotations to emphasize especially interesting features of the data that are being displayed (the annotations are cleared when the users moves from year to year). Finally, there are tooltips on nearly all the visualizations that give the user more detail. These are presented upon a mouseover event and removed upon a mouseout event.

Parameters

The use of parameters are most effectively demonstrated on the murder map scene. Here, the parameter of year is used to filter each murder event that is being displayed on the map. In addition, we could consider the scale of the map elements to be a parameters that varies as the viewer zooms and pans the map.

Triggers

Again, the use of triggers are best demonstrated on the murder map scene. Below the map is a slider that allows the user to change the horizontal position of the handle. By doing so, this event triggers the map to redraw the murder events that are equal or less than the year indicated by the slider. In addition, the user can scroll and click on the map which triggers the map to be redrawn according to the appropriate scale.

Data

The data for this project was sourced from the Denver Open Data Catalog and was cleaned and manipulated with python.
